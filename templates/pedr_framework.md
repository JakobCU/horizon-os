# PEDR Framework — Impact Pathway Template

> **Used by**: Impact Specialist
> **Source**: Horizon Europe impact guidance — "Pathway to Impact" methodology
> **Purpose**: Structure the impact narrative for Part B Section 2.1

---

## What is PEDR?

PEDR = **Pathway → Effect → Diffusion → Result**

It is the recommended framework for describing how project outputs lead to real-world impact. Each key exploitable result (KER) should have its own PEDR chain.

## PEDR Chain Template

For each Key Exploitable Result (KER):

### KER [N]: [Name of Result]

| Stage | Description | Timeline |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| **Pathway** | What the project will produce (output/deliverable) and the concrete steps to make it available to target users | During project (M1-M[end]) |
| **Effect** | The measurable change that occurs when target users adopt/use the result | Short-term (0-2 years post-project) |
| **Diffusion** | How the result spreads beyond initial users — scaling, replication, standardization, policy uptake | Medium-term (2-5 years post-project) |
| **Result** | The wider societal, economic, or scientific impact achieved at scale | Long-term (5-10 years post-project) |

### Detailed PEDR per KER

```
KER [N]: [Result Name]
  Owner: [Partner acronym]
  Type: [Software / Methodology / Standard / Policy recommendation / Dataset / Training programme]
  TRL at end of project: [X]

  PATHWAY:
    Output: [What is produced — e.g., "validated NLP toolkit for multilingual threat detection"]
    Target users: [Who will use it — e.g., "EU LEAs, Europol, national border agencies"]
    Transfer mechanism: [How it reaches users — e.g., "open-source release, training workshops, integration with existing LEA platforms"]
    Barriers: [What might prevent uptake — e.g., "data sensitivity, procurement cycles, interoperability"]
    Mitigation: [How barriers will be addressed]

  EFFECT:
    Expected change: [What changes for the user — e.g., "30% reduction in manual translation workload for cross-border investigations"]
    KPIs: [Measurable indicators]
    Evidence plan: [How you will measure this — pilot evaluations, user surveys, adoption metrics]

  DIFFUSION:
    Scaling strategy: [Beyond pilot — e.g., "deployment via EU-LISA infrastructure", "standardization via CEN/CENELEC"]
    Replication: [Can other regions/sectors use this? How?]
    Policy uptake: [Will results inform EU/national policy?]
    Sustainability: [Business model, open-source community, institutional adoption]

  RESULT:
    Societal impact: [Long-term benefit — e.g., "enhanced EU internal security through faster cross-border intelligence sharing"]
    Economic impact: [Job creation, cost savings, market creation]
    Scientific impact: [New knowledge, methodological advances, open datasets]
```

## Impact Dimensions (Horizon Europe)

Every proposal should address three impact dimensions:

### 1. Scientific Impact
- New knowledge, methods, or data
- Publications and open science contributions
- Training of researchers (PhDs, postdocs)

### 2. Societal Impact
- Contribution to EU security, safety, resilience
- Fundamental rights protection
- Public trust in security technologies
- Environmental sustainability (link to DNSH)

### 3. Economic Impact
- Innovation and market creation
- SME growth and competitiveness
- Standardization and interoperability
- Cost savings for public sector

## Tips for Strong Impact Sections

1. **Be specific, not generic** — "improved security" is weak; "30% faster cross-border data exchange for 12 EU border agencies" is strong
2. **Name your users** — Don't say "stakeholders"; say "Europol's AP Travellers focal point, Spanish National Police CITCO unit"
3. **Quantify where possible** — Even estimates are better than vague claims
4. **Show the causal chain** — Don't just list impacts; explain WHY and HOW they will happen
5. **Address barriers honestly** — Evaluators respect realistic assessments of adoption challenges
6. **Link to the call text** — Use the call's "Expected Outcomes" as your impact targets and mirror the language
