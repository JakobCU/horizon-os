## Differentiation Strategy

| Dimension | Likely Competitor Approach | Our Approach (CORTEX) | Why Ours is Stronger |
|-----------|--------------------------|----------------------|---------------------|
| Technical angle | Bigger/faster robots, more sensors, better drone autonomy | Human-AI teaming layer: biosignal-driven adaptive autonomy + adaptive C2 | Novel — nobody occupies this space; complements rather than competes with existing robot platforms |
| Core thesis | "Better robots save more lives" | "Better-supported humans, teamed with robots, save more lives" | Aligns with EU human-centric AI values and AI Act; resonates with end-user skepticism of full autonomy |
| End-user engagement | End-users as validation partners (test at the end) | End-users as co-designers from M1; advisory autonomy (human confirms); works council engagement | Deeper buy-in; addresses adoption barrier that killed uptake of past projects |
| Innovation level | Hardware innovation (new platforms) | Middleware innovation (platform-agnostic software layer) | Lower adoption barrier; works with existing infrastructure; higher exploitation potential |
| Exploitation | Requires buying new hardware | Software overlay for existing C2 + PPE-embedded sensors | Public-sector-budget-friendly; no fleet replacement needed |
| AI Act compliance | Often silent or bolt-on | Designed for compliance by architecture — human-in-the-loop, advisory mode, transparency | Growing regulatory scrutiny makes this a competitive advantage |
| Measurables | "We flew a drone in a building" | "We reduced cognitive overload incidents by X%, decision latency by Y seconds, demonstrated zero-manual-switching adaptive autonomy" | Concrete human performance metrics vs. technology demos |
